Structures
The structure subsystem is a housing in which all the components are mounted.It has under its aegis the physical units comprising the body of the satellite. The broad function of this group is to design the body of the satellite and mount all components on it, shielding them well from shock, excessive temperatures, radiation and stray magnetic fields, control the internal thermal environment at all times and design mechanisms crucial to the working of components. Responsibilities of structure subsystem:
  • Provide the support to all other subsystems and materialize the geometry of the spacecraft and its payloads by simulating loading conditions during launch in orbit.
  • Guarantee the necessary strength to survive all phases of the spacecraft life without failures.
  • Keep the structural stiffness in certain limits to guarantee the operational functionality of the overall system and avoid coupled resonant responses.
  • To allocate resources of weight and dimension to all other teams.
  • To provide means of mounting onboard, fastening and cushioning all components securely.
  • To design a stable structure with moments conforming to the requirements dictated by the Attitude Control group.
  • To design mechanisms for the smooth deployment of booms and antennae and facilitate communication by rotating the antennae to point towards the ground station.
  • To provide routing for the various power and data cables used, interface all components to each other and interface the satellite to the launch vehicle.
  • To fabricate the prototype and final flight models of the satellite.
  • To test the satellite model for resistance to shocks and thermal vacuum conditions.
ADCS

The Attitude Determination and Control Subsystem (ADCS) is responsible for both sensing and controlling the spacecraft attitude. The team creates a simulation of the spacecraft dynamics and writes the Kalman filter and control software used to steer the satellite.

For complete success of a satellite mission the satellite has to be maneuvered using attitude control dynamics throughout its life beginning from the separation from the launch vehicle upto the end of life. Several forces act on the satellite that cause the disturbance in its platform. Attitude control is essential for the satellite to prevent it from tumbling and for obtaining pointing accuracy. Basic attitude control subsystem comprises: an ability to sense the current attitude of the satellite, an ability to compute the errors between the current attitude and the desired attitude, and some mechanism/algorithm by which the attitude error can be controlled and corrected.

Satellite is not a rigid body but has flexible appendages in the form of solar panels and antennaes. Some parts will hence bend and vibrate under the actions of forces which when combined with the inertia of the spacecraft mass will produce transients in the spacecraft performance which can last several seconds or minutes.

The primary objectives of the Attitude Control subsystem are
1) Control the initial random angular motion of the satellite.
2) Attain a favorable configuration suitable for the communication/optical payload
3) Minimize the power consumption.

Communication

The primary goal of the communication subsystem is to provide a link to relay data findings and send commands to and from the NanoSat. Telemetry and command subsystems will ensure continuous communication between the ground station and the NanoSat after ejection from the rocket.

Analog and digital data collected by the sensors and payload of the satellite must be relayed to the ground station via the telemetry system, which is composed of a transmitter that acts much like a “modem in a computer”

The satellite has an uplink in the satellite segment of the UHF amateur radio frequency band (435-438 MHz) and a downlink in the satellite segment of the VHF amateur radio frequency band (145.8-146.0 MHz).

The communications consists of two Radio Amateur Platforms (RAPs) which are almost identical, except that the primary has a linear transponder and the secondary not. The RAPs are built using standard commercial-off-the-shelf components.The linear transponder will relay a 40 KHz wide passband from the UHF band to the VHF band. This provides a very flexible system, which can be used by amateur radio operators worldwide using CW, SSB and PSK31 modulation.

The antenna subsystem consists of a two canted turnstile arrangements, one for each band. The antennas are fed in quadrature phase in order to achieve the desired pattern and polarization by means of a phasing circuit, which connects to both transceivers. The antennas are stowed in launch configuration in a modular antenna box. The eight antenna are safely deployed in orbit, by melting a restraining wire on command by the onboard computer.

On Board Computer

The term “On Board Computer” indicates, rather obviously, any unit flying on board a satellite which provides processing capability. However, the On Board Computer, or OBC, more commonly refers to as the computer of the satellite’s avionic sub-system, i.e. the unit where the On Board Software run. In turn, the “On Board Software”, despite the rather generic label, is known as the software implementing the satellite’s vital functions such as: attitude and orbit control in both nominal and non-nominal cases, telecommands execution or dispatching, housekeeping telemetry gathering and formatting, on board time synchronisation and distribution, failure detection, isolation and recovery, etc.

Based on the above, the very essence of an OBC is the microprocessor board, consisting of microprocessor, non-volatile memories, volatile memories and the companion chip that connects the microprocessor to different peripherals.

However, in space as well as in the consumer market, as the shrinking of the electronic components enables it, the trend is towards more dense and integrated systems, aiming at continuous reduction of volume, mass, and power consumption.

A modern On Board Computers, in fact, not only provides the aforementioned processing resources, but it does include other functions in the same box such as:

  • DC/DC Power conversion and regulation
  • Ground Telecommand Decoding
  • Packet Telemetry Formatting
  • On Board time management
  • Autonomous Reconfiguration
  • Local Mass Memory function
  • Housekeeping telemetry
  • Interfacing with other Avionics subsystems
